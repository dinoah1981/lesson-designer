# History Lesson Structures: Validated Templates for Automated Design

**Domain:** Evidence-based instructional sequences for history/social studies
**Purpose:** Provide specific, actionable lesson structures for automated lesson designer
**Confidence:** HIGH (based on validated curricula with documented implementations)

## Core Principle

History lessons should follow an **inquiry arc** rather than traditional "lecture → practice → assessment" structure. Students learn historical thinking by *doing* historical thinking, not by listening to historical narratives.

## Universal Lesson Components (All History Lessons)

Every effective history lesson includes these elements, regardless of specific structure:

1. **Compelling Question** - Genuinely open-ended, debatable, addresses enduring issues
2. **Just-in-Time Background** - Essential context delivered immediately before inquiry (not front-loaded lecture)
3. **Primary Source Analysis** - Students examine 2-7 documents (quantity varies by level)
4. **Explicit Strategy Instruction** - Teacher models historical thinking processes via think-aloud
5. **Structured Collaboration** - Pairs or small groups discuss before individual work
6. **Evidence-Based Conclusion** - Written or oral argument supported by source evidence
7. **Metacognitive Reflection** - Debrief on thinking process, not just content learned

## Template 1: Standard Document-Based Inquiry (50-60 minute period)

**Source:** Reading Like a Historian curriculum (Stanford/Digital Inquiry Group)
**Use case:** Daily history lessons, grades 9-12
**Document quantity:** 3-4 for novices, 4-6 for advanced
**Confidence:** HIGH (101 validated lessons, used worldwide)

### Timing Breakdown (50-minute period)

| Activity | Time | Purpose |
|----------|------|---------|
| Hook | 5 min | Engage students, introduce compelling question |
| Background Essay | 8-10 min | Provide essential context via reading or mini-lecture |
| Document 1: Teacher Model | 5-7 min | Think-aloud demonstrating sourcing, contextualization, corroboration, close reading |
| Document 2: Guided Practice | 8-10 min | Teacher scaffolds with questions, students respond |
| Documents 3-4: Collaborative Work | 12-15 min | Pairs analyze remaining documents with graphic organizer |
| Synthesis Discussion or Writing | 8-10 min | Answer compelling question using evidence |
| Exit Ticket / Reflection | 3-5 min | Formative assessment on one thinking skill |

### Detailed Steps

**Step 1: Hook (5 minutes)**
- Purpose: Create cognitive dissonance or curiosity
- Methods:
  - Show provocative image and ask "What's happening here? When? Why?"
  - Present brief scenario that challenges assumptions
  - Share conflicting statistics or quotes
- Output: State compelling question clearly (write on board, show on slide)
- Example: "You'll see two accounts of the Boston Massacre. One is from a British officer, one from a colonial merchant. To what extent was this event a 'massacre'?"

**Step 2: Background Essay (8-10 minutes)**
- Purpose: Provide minimum context needed to understand documents
- Format: 300-500 word reading OR 5-7 minute mini-lecture
- Content requirements:
  - When and where event occurred
  - Key people/groups involved (2-4 maximum)
  - Essential facts students won't know
  - Critical vocabulary (3-5 terms maximum)
- What NOT to include:
  - Comprehensive coverage of topic
  - Conclusions students should reach
  - Detailed narratives
- Delivery:
  - Option A: Independent reading with 2-3 comprehension questions
  - Option B: Teacher presents orally with visual support (timeline, map)
  - Option C: Pair reading with Think-Pair-Share discussion

**Step 3: Document 1 - Teacher Think-Aloud (5-7 minutes)**
- Purpose: Make historical thinking visible
- Process:
  1. Display document (project or handout)
  2. Read aloud while students follow
  3. Think aloud throughout:
     - **Sourcing:** "I notice this was written by... in [year]. I'm wondering if their position as [role] might influence what they say..."
     - **Contextualization:** "This was right after [event], so they probably knew about... but might not have known [future outcome]..."
     - **Close Reading:** "The phrase '[specific quote]' suggests they believed... The word choice 'massacre' is interesting because..."
     - **Corroboration:** (set up for later) "I'll want to see if other sources agree with this claim about..."
  4. Complete graphic organizer section for this document
- Teacher explicitly names strategies: "What I just did is called sourcing - evaluating where this document came from to understand its perspective."

**Step 4: Document 2 - Guided Practice (8-10 minutes)**
- Purpose: Scaffold student practice with support
- Process:
  1. Students read document independently (2-3 min)
  2. Teacher asks guiding questions, students respond:
     - "Who created this document?" (sourcing)
     - "What was happening at this time that might be important?" (contextualization)
     - "How does this account compare to Document 1?" (corroboration)
     - "What language reveals the author's view?" (close reading)
  3. Students complete graphic organizer section with guidance
  4. Volunteers share answers, teacher validates or redirects
- Teacher gradually reduces support (asks questions rather than models)

**Step 5: Documents 3-4 - Collaborative Analysis (12-15 minutes)**
- Purpose: Independent practice with peer support
- Process:
  1. Assign pairs (mixed-ability if possible)
  2. Each pair analyzes remaining documents using graphic organizer
  3. Teacher circulates, listens, provides targeted feedback:
     - Catch students doing thinking skills correctly: "I heard you say the author worked for the British government - that's sourcing. How might that affect what they wrote?"
     - Scaffold when stuck: "Look at the date again. What had just happened? What might the author have known or not known?"
  4. Optional: Pairs complete different documents, then teach each other (jigsaw)

**Step 6: Synthesis (8-10 minutes)**
- Purpose: Use evidence to answer compelling question
- Format Options:

  **Option A: Quick Write**
  - Prompt: "Answer our compelling question in a paragraph using evidence from at least 2 documents."
  - Students write independently (6 min)
  - 2-3 volunteers share (2-4 min)

  **Option B: Think-Pair-Share**
  - Think: Individual brainstorm (2 min)
  - Pair: Discuss answer with partner (3-4 min)
  - Share: Class discussion (3-4 min)
  - Teacher charts different positions on board

  **Option C: Four Corners**
  - Post answer options in room corners (e.g., "Definitely a massacre," "Somewhat a massacre," "Not a massacre," "Impossible to determine")
  - Students move to corners matching their answer
  - Corner groups discuss reasoning (3 min)
  - Groups share (5 min)

**Step 7: Exit Ticket (3-5 minutes)**
- Purpose: Formative assessment
- Format: Brief written response
- Focus Options:
  - Assess one thinking skill: "Which document was most reliable for answering our question? Explain your reasoning." (tests sourcing)
  - Content check: "What's one new thing you learned? One question you still have?"
  - Metacognitive: "Which thinking strategy was hardest today? Why?"

### Materials Needed

- Compelling question (written clearly, visible to all)
- Background essay (300-500 words, reading-level appropriate)
- 3-4 primary source documents (excerpted to 150-300 words each)
- Graphic organizer (sourcing chart, corroboration matrix, or combined)
- Exit ticket (half-sheet or digital form)
- Optional: Images for hook, timeline for background, vocabulary support

### Differentiation Points

- **Reading support:** Provide vocabulary definitions in margins, excerpt to shorter passages (100-150 words), partner reading
- **Advanced extension:** Add 5th document with more complex language, require outside evidence in synthesis, ask for historiographical analysis
- **Scaffolding:** Provide sentence starters for synthesis ("According to [Source A], ... However, [Source B] suggests..."), partially completed graphic organizer
- **Time management:** Novices may need 2 periods (more time on documents), advanced learners can handle in 1 period

## Template 2: Full DBQ Sequence (2-3 periods)

**Source:** DBQ Project Method
**Use case:** Major assessments, AP preparation, complex inquiries
**Document quantity:** 6-7 documents
**Confidence:** HIGH (validated method, widely adopted)

### The Six-Step DBQ Process

**Step 1: The Hook (5-10 minutes)**
- Purpose: Engage and orient to question
- More elaborate than standard lesson hook
- Examples:
  - Brief video clip
  - Image analysis activity
  - Provocative quote with Turn & Talk
  - Mini-debate on related contemporary issue
- Must connect to historical question students will investigate

**Step 2: Background Essay (15-20 minutes)**
- Purpose: Provide robust historical context
- Format: 500-800 word essay (more substantial than daily lesson)
- Students read and annotate
- Includes:
  - Timeline of key events
  - Essential people, places, concepts
  - Historical context for all documents
  - Does NOT answer the DBQ question
- Follow-up: Quick quiz or discussion to ensure comprehension

**Step 3: Understanding the Question (10-15 minutes)**
- Purpose: Ensure students understand what's being asked
- Activities:
  - Underline key terms in prompt
  - Rephrase question in own words
  - Brainstorm what knowledge/evidence would help answer
  - Review rubric criteria
- Clarify task: "You'll need to use at least 6 documents and develop a thesis..."

**Step 4: Document Analysis (60-90 minutes total, may span 2 days)**
- Purpose: Deep engagement with sources
- Process:
  1. Teacher models first document (think-aloud)
  2. Students analyze remaining 5-6 documents
  3. Use comprehensive graphic organizer:
     - Source information (author, date, type, purpose, audience)
     - Summary of main point
     - Evidence useful for answering question
     - Perspective/bias/reliability notes
- Pacing:
  - Day 1: Hook, Background Essay, Understanding Question, analyze 3 documents
  - Day 2: Analyze remaining 3-4 documents

**Step 5: Bucketing, Chickenfoot & Thrash-Out (20-30 minutes)**
- Purpose: Organize evidence before writing

  **Bucketing (10-12 min):**
  - Categorize evidence into 2-4 themes/arguments ("buckets")
  - Example for "Was imperialism justified?" might have buckets: Economic factors, Moral arguments, Political considerations
  - Students sort document evidence into buckets

  **Chickenfoot (5 min):**
  - Visual organizer: Draw large "Y" shape
  - Top of Y = thesis statement
  - Each branch = one bucket with supporting evidence
  - Creates essay outline

  **Thrash-Out (10-12 min):**
  - Pair discussion: Students verbally rehearse argument
  - Partner asks questions, challenges reasoning
  - Refines thinking before writing

**Step 6: The Essay (45-60 minutes)**
- Purpose: Evidence-based written argument
- Format: Multi-paragraph essay (4-5 paragraphs typical)
- Structure:
  - Introduction: Context + thesis
  - Body paragraphs: One per bucket, each using 2-3 documents as evidence
  - Conclusion: Synthesis
- During writing:
  - Teacher circulates for quick conferences
  - Students reference graphic organizers and outline
- After writing:
  - Optional: Peer review using rubric
  - Self-assessment against criteria

### Timing by Day

**Day 1 (50-60 minutes):**
- Hook (5-10 min)
- Background Essay reading and discussion (15-20 min)
- Understanding the Question (10 min)
- Begin Document Analysis - complete 2-3 documents (20-25 min)

**Day 2 (50-60 minutes):**
- Review (5 min)
- Complete Document Analysis - remaining 3-4 documents (25-30 min)
- Bucketing (10 min)
- Chickenfoot outline (5 min)
- Begin Thrash-Out (5-10 min)

**Day 3 (50-60 minutes):**
- Complete Thrash-Out (5-10 min)
- Essay writing (40-50 min)

**Condensed Version (2 periods for experienced students):**
- Day 1: Hook → Background → Question Understanding → All document analysis (homework: finish documents)
- Day 2: Bucketing → Chickenfoot → Thrash-Out → Essay writing

### Assessment Rubric (6-Point College Board Model)

| Criterion | Points | Requirements |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| Thesis/Claim | 0-1 | Historically defensible thesis establishing line of reasoning |
| Contextualization | 0-1 | Describes broader historical context relevant to prompt |
| Evidence from Documents | 0-2 | Uses 3 docs (1 pt) OR uses 6 docs to support argument (2 pts) |
| Evidence Beyond Documents | 0-1 | Specific historical evidence not found in documents |
| Analysis and Reasoning | 0-1 | Explains how/why doc's POV, purpose, situation, or audience is relevant (for 3+ docs) |

**Total: 6 points**

### Materials Needed

- DBQ packet:
  - Historical background essay (500-800 words)
  - 6-7 primary source documents (excerpted)
  - Document analysis graphic organizer
  - Bucketing worksheet
  - Essay outline (chickenfoot template)
- Rubric (provide to students before writing)
- Optional: Sample DBQ response for modeling

## Template 3: Structured Academic Controversy (2 periods)

**Source:** David & Roger Johnson (University of Minnesota), adapted for history
**Use case:** Controversial historical questions, developing perspective-taking
**Document quantity:** 4-6 documents (2-3 per position)
**Confidence:** HIGH (empirical research validation, detailed protocol)

### Overview

SAC shifts debate goal from "winning" to "understanding multiple perspectives and synthesizing." Students argue positions they may not personally hold, then abandon those positions to reach consensus.

### Structure

**Four-person teams:** Two dyads (pairs), each initially assigned opposing position

**Five phases:**
1. Preparation (homework or Day 1)
2. Presentation Round 1 (dyad A presents, dyad B listens)
3. Presentation Round 2 (roles reverse)
4. Perspective Switch (optional: dyads argue opposite position)
5. Consensus Building (team works together)

### Detailed Process

**Preparation Phase (30-40 minutes - can be homework)**
- Controversial question framed: "Was Abraham Lincoln a racist?" or "Were atomic bombings of Japan justified?"
- Each dyad receives 2-3 documents supporting one position
- Dyads read sources, identify:
  - Main arguments
  - Strongest evidence
  - Author perspectives
- Prepare 5-minute presentation of position (can use notes, quotes, visuals)

**Day 1: Presentations and Active Listening (50-60 minutes)**

*Setup (5 min):*
- Arrange desks for 4-person teams (2 dyads facing each other)
- Explain SAC process and ground rules:
  - Listeners do not interrupt
  - Must restate other position before presenting own
  - Goal is understanding, not winning

*Round 1 (12-15 min):*
- Dyad A presents Position 1 (5-7 min)
- Dyad B listens, takes notes, does NOT respond
- Dyad B restates what they heard (3-5 min): "You argued that... Your evidence included... You believe..."
- Dyad A confirms or clarifies
- Brief questions for clarification only (2-3 min)

*Round 2 (12-15 min):*
- Dyad B presents Position 2 (5-7 min)
- Dyad A listens, takes notes
- Dyad A restates (3-5 min)
- Dyad B confirms or clarifies
- Brief questions (2-3 min)

*Open Discussion (15-20 min):*
- All four team members discuss
- Guiding questions:
  - "What was the strongest evidence for Position 1? For Position 2?"
  - "What did each side miss or overlook?"
  - "How do historians who take Position 1 explain [specific evidence]?"
- Teacher circulates, listens, asks probing questions
- No consensus yet - just exploring complexity

*Homework:*
- Each student writes reflection: "What's strongest about the position I didn't present? What's weakest about the position I did present?"

**Day 2: Consensus Building (50-60 minutes)**

*Abandon Positions (5 min):*
- Explicit instruction: "You are no longer assigned to a position. Your job now is to work together to figure out what the evidence best supports."
- Dyads shuffle seats (sit mixed, not across from partner)

*Consensus Work (30-35 min):*
- Team of 4 works together
- Task: "Reach agreement on answer to question, OR clearly identify where/why you disagree"
- Process:
  - Review all 4-6 documents together
  - Identify points of agreement among sources
  - Identify contradictions or gaps
  - Develop team position statement (2-3 sentences)
  - Prepare justification using evidence
- Teacher prompts:
  - "Can you find middle ground?"
  - "Maybe it's not either/or - what if both are partly right?"
  - "What would a historian need to know to fully answer this?"

*Share-Out (15-20 min):*
- Each team presents consensus statement (2-3 min per team)
- Class notes common themes
- Teacher highlights how teams synthesized beyond initial positions

*Reflection (5 min):*
- Individual writing:
  - "How did your thinking change during this activity?"
  - "What was hardest about considering the other perspective?"
  - "How is understanding different from agreeing?"

### Variations

**Perspective Switch (adds 20-30 minutes on Day 1):**
- After both presentations, dyads argue the OPPOSITE position
- Forces deeper engagement with all evidence
- Use when you have advanced students or extra time

**Written Individual Conclusion (adds 30-40 minutes - could be homework):**
- After consensus building, students write individual essays
- Prompt: "Answer the question using evidence from all sources. Your answer may differ from your team's consensus."
- Demonstrates personal synthesis informed by collaborative work

### Materials Needed

- Controversial historical question (clearly stated)
- Document packets:
  - Position 1: 2-3 sources
  - Position 2: 2-3 sources
- Presentation planning sheet (for dyads)
- Active listening guide (for note-taking during presentations)
- Consensus worksheet (for Day 2)
- Reflection prompts

### Assessment Options

- **Formative:** Observe discussions, use listening guide rubric
- **Summative options:**
  - Individual written response after consensus (assesses synthesis)
  - Reflection essay on perspective-taking
  - Team consensus statement with justification
- Focus assessment on:
  - Evidence use (citing sources)
  - Acknowledging complexity (not simplistic either/or)
  - Perspective-taking (understanding positions they disagree with)

## Template 4: Historical Empathy Lesson (50-60 minutes)

**Source:** Historical empathy pedagogy research (2025-2026)
**Use case:** Understanding historical actors' decisions, avoiding presentism
**Document quantity:** 2-3 documents (focused on perspectives, not quantity)
**Confidence:** MEDIUM-HIGH (growing research base, demonstrated effectiveness)

### Purpose

Historical empathy means **understanding why people in the past acted as they did given their context, knowledge, and beliefs** - WITHOUT necessarily agreeing with their actions. Prevents presentism (judging past by modern values).

### Structure

1. **Hook: Cognitive Dissonance** (5-8 min)
2. **Context Building** (12-15 min)
3. **Perspective Source Analysis** (15-18 min)
4. **Empathy Activity** (12-15 min)
5. **Debrief: Understanding vs. Agreement** (5-8 min)

### Detailed Steps

**Step 1: Hook - Create Cognitive Dissonance (5-8 minutes)**
- Present historical action that seems wrong/strange to modern students
- Examples:
  - Colonists destroying property (Boston Tea Party) - isn't that vandalism?
  - Washington owning slaves while fighting for liberty - hypocrisy?
  - Women not fighting for voting rights earlier - why wait so long?
- Quick poll: "Do you think this was justified?" (show of hands)
- Pose question: "Why might people at the time have seen this differently?"

**Step 2: Context Building (12-15 minutes)**
- Purpose: Establish what people at the time knew, believed, and could do
- Deliver via mini-lecture, timeline activity, or reading
- Three essential context types:

  **Temporal Context:**
  - What had already happened? (they knew this)
  - What hadn't happened yet? (they didn't know this)
  - Example: "Colonists didn't know the Revolution would succeed. From their view, they were risking execution for treason."

  **Belief Systems:**
  - What did most people believe about [topic]?
  - What alternatives existed? Were they widely known?
  - Example: "Most Europeans believed in racial hierarchies as scientific fact, not just opinion."

  **Constraints and Options:**
  - What choices did people actually have?
  - What would be the consequences of different actions?
  - Example: "Women who advocated for voting rights risked social ostracism, job loss, even arrest."

- Activity: Create "What They Knew / Didn't Know" chart
- Emphasize: "We're not deciding if this is right. We're figuring out how it made sense to them."

**Step 3: Perspective Source Analysis (15-18 minutes)**
- Select 2-3 primary sources FROM historical actors explaining their reasoning
- Not sources ABOUT them - sources BY them
- Students analyze:
  - What reasons do they give for their actions?
  - What do they assume or believe?
  - What are they trying to achieve?
  - What constraints or fears do they mention?
- Use modified graphic organizer focused on perspective:
  - "Actor's stated reasoning"
  - "Beliefs/assumptions revealed"
  - "What they're responding to (context)"

**Step 4: Empathy Activity (12-15 minutes)**

Choose ONE of these activities:

**Option A: First-Person Perspective Writing**
- Prompt: "You are [historical actor] in [year]. Write a letter to a friend explaining why you are [taking this action]. Use what you know about the time period."
- Requirements:
  - Reference historical context
  - Explain reasoning from their viewpoint
  - Show awareness of alternatives and why they were rejected
- Example: "Write a letter as a colonist in 1773 explaining to your British cousin why you participated in the Boston Tea Party."

**Option B: Decision-Making Role-Play**
- Students in small groups (3-4)
- Scenario: Historical actors facing decision
- Provided: Context cards, constraint cards, consequence cards
- Task: Discuss options and decide, staying in historical perspective
- Groups share decisions and reasoning

**Option C: Perspective Comparison**
- Two contrasting perspectives on same event
- Students write: "From [Person A]'s perspective... From [Person B]'s perspective..."
- Emphasize: Both can be understandable given different contexts, even if both aren't right

**Step 5: Debrief - Understanding vs. Agreement (5-8 minutes)**
- CRITICAL component - prevents moral relativism
- Discussion questions:
  - "Can you understand why someone did something without agreeing it was right?"
  - "Does understanding historical context excuse actions that harmed people?"
  - "How is historical empathy different from moral judgment?"
- Establish clearly:
  - Understanding ≠ endorsing
  - Context explains but doesn't justify everything
  - Historians seek to understand; ethical judgment is separate
- Reflection prompt: "What's one thing you understand about [historical actor] now that you didn't before? Does understanding change your evaluation of their actions?"

### Special Considerations

**Topics requiring extreme care:**
- Slavery, genocide, war crimes, oppression
- Risk: Trivializing suffering or appearing to excuse injustice
- Approach:
  - Focus empathy on victims' experiences and resistance, not just perpetrators
  - Acknowledge harm explicitly
  - Frame as "understand the system/ideology" not "justify actions"
  - Example: Study how enslaved people maintained humanity and resisted, plus how enslavers rationalized (to understand how the system persisted), NOT to justify slavery

**Avoiding stereotype reinforcement:**
- Don't essentialize: "Women in the 1800s thought X" → "Some women in the 1800s thought X"
- Show diversity within groups
- Use multiple perspectives from same demographic

### Materials Needed

- Hook material (image, quote, scenario)
- Context information:
  - Timeline of relevant events
  - Belief systems explanation
  - Constraints/options description
- 2-3 primary sources showing historical actors' perspectives
- Graphic organizer for perspective analysis
- Activity materials:
  - Writing prompt for first-person writing OR
  - Decision scenario with context/constraint cards OR
  - Perspective comparison template
- Reflection questions

### Assessment

**Formative:**
- Monitor during activity: Are students referencing context? Using historical reasoning?
- Check for presentism: Catch statements like "They should have known better" → redirect to "What did they know at the time?"

**Summative:**
- Evaluate perspective writing/role-play for:
  - Historical accuracy of context references
  - Understanding of historical actor's reasoning
  - Avoiding anachronism (using only knowledge available at the time)
- Reflection response assessing:
  - Can student distinguish understanding from agreement?
  - Do they recognize value of historical empathy?

## Template 5: Inquiry-Based Investigation (3-5 days)

**Source:** C3 Framework (College, Career, and Civic Life)
**Use case:** Extended investigations, project-based learning, authentic research
**Document quantity:** Variable (students may find own sources)
**Confidence:** HIGH (NCSS framework, widely adopted)

### The Four Dimensions of C3

**Dimension 1: Developing Questions and Planning Inquiries**
**Dimension 2: Applying Disciplinary Concepts and Tools**
**Dimension 3: Evaluating Sources and Using Evidence**
**Dimension 4: Communicating Conclusions and Taking Informed Action**

### Multi-Day Structure

**Day 1: Developing Questions (50-60 minutes)**

*Compelling Question Introduction (10 min):*
- Present open-ended question addressing enduring issue
- Examples:
  - "How should we remember Christopher Columbus?"
  - "When is revolution justified?"
  - "Does economic growth require inequality?"
- Connect to students' lives and contemporary issues

*Supporting Questions Development (20 min):*
- Teacher provides 2-3 supporting questions, students generate 1-2 more
- Supporting questions are more specific, scaffold the compelling question
- Example for "How should we remember Columbus?":
  - What did Columbus accomplish? (description)
  - What were the consequences of Columbus's voyages for different groups? (analysis)
  - How have historians' interpretations of Columbus changed over time? (historiography)
  - What criteria should we use for commemorating historical figures? (evaluation)

*Inquiry Planning (20 min):*
- What do we already know about this topic?
- What do we need to find out?
- What sources might help us answer these questions?
- Create inquiry plan (graphic organizer)

**Days 2-3: Investigating (100-120 minutes total)**

*Source Collection and Analysis:*
- Teacher provides core document set (6-10 sources)
- Students may find additional sources (guided by supporting questions)
- Analysis activities:
  - Annotate sources for evidence relevant to supporting questions
  - Complete source analysis for each document
  - Take notes organized by supporting question

*Applying Disciplinary Tools (Dimension 2):*
- Use historical thinking skills (sourcing, contextualization, corroboration, close reading)
- Apply concepts: causation, continuity/change, context, perspective
- Create evidence charts connecting sources to supporting questions

*Teacher Role:*
- Circulate, conference with individuals/small groups
- Scaffold research process
- Ask probing questions:
  - "How does this source help answer your supporting question?"
  - "What perspective is missing from your sources?"
  - "How do Sources A and C relate to each other?"

**Day 4: Developing Claims and Arguments (50-60 minutes)**

*Evidence Synthesis (25-30 min):*
- Review all sources
- For each supporting question, what does evidence suggest?
- Identify patterns, contradictions, gaps
- Develop mini-claims for each supporting question

*Compelling Question Response (20-25 min):*
- Use supporting question answers to address compelling question
- Develop thesis/claim
- Outline argument structure
- Identify strongest evidence

**Day 5: Communicating Conclusions (50-60 minutes)**

*Format Options (students choose or teacher assigns):*

**Option A: Written Essay**
- Multi-paragraph argument
- Introduction addresses compelling question
- Body paragraphs address supporting questions
- Conclusion synthesizes

**Option B: Multimedia Presentation**
- Slides, video, podcast, infographic
- Must include visual evidence from sources
- Addresses compelling and supporting questions

**Option C: Deliberation or Socratic Seminar**
- Structured discussion
- Students defend positions with evidence
- Respond to counterarguments

**Option D: Informed Action (Dimension 4 - optional)**
- If compelling question connects to contemporary issue
- Students propose action based on historical understanding
- Examples:
  - Letter to editor applying historical lessons
  - Proposal for memorial/commemoration
  - Policy recommendation informed by historical analysis

*Reflection Component:*
- Metacognitive: "What was your research process? What worked well? What would you do differently?"
- Content: "How did historical evidence shape your answer?"
- Connection: "How does this inquiry relate to issues today?"

### Materials Needed

- Compelling question (clearly stated)
- 3-4 supporting questions (teacher-provided, may add student-generated)
- Document set (6-10 sources minimum)
  - Mix of primary and secondary sources
  - Multiple perspectives
  - Various formats (text, image, chart, map)
- Research planning organizer
- Evidence tracking charts (by supporting question)
- Argument outline template
- Rubric for final product
- Reflection prompts

### Assessment

**Formative Throughout:**
- Day 1: Check inquiry plan for understanding
- Days 2-3: Review evidence charts, conference notes
- Day 4: Review argument outlines, provide feedback

**Summative:**
- Final product (essay, presentation, etc.) evaluated on:
  - Compelling question addressed (thesis/claim)
  - Supporting questions answered with evidence
  - Source analysis (sourcing, contextualization, corroboration)
  - Argument quality (logic, evidence, synthesis)
  - Communication (clarity, organization)
- Rubric aligns to C3 dimensions

**Reflection:**
- Inquiry process reflection (metacognitive)
- Content learning reflection (what did you learn?)

## Lesson Structure Decision Tree

**For automated lesson designer: Which template to use?**

```
START: What's the instructional goal?

├─ Daily lesson (1 period), building historical thinking skills
│  → Use Template 1: Standard Document-Based Inquiry
│
├─ Major assessment or AP preparation
│  └─ How much time available?
│      ├─ 2-3 periods → Use Template 2: Full DBQ Sequence
│      └─ 1 period (timed practice) → Use Template 1 with 6-7 docs
│
├─ Controversial question with multiple defensible positions
│  └─ Do students need to deeply understand opposing views?
│      ├─ Yes → Use Template 3: Structured Academic Controversy
│      └─ No → Use Template 1 or 2 (standard inquiry adequate)
│
├─ Understanding historical actors' decisions/perspectives
│  └─ Is avoiding presentism a key goal?
│      ├─ Yes → Use Template 4: Historical Empathy Lesson
│      └─ No → Use Template 1 (can include perspective analysis)
│
└─ Extended investigation (3+ days), student-driven research
   → Use Template 5: C3 Inquiry-Based Investigation
```

## Cross-Template Elements

**All templates share these instructional principles:**

1. **Questions before answers** - Start with compelling question, not lecture
2. **Just-in-time context** - Background when needed, not front-loaded
3. **Explicit strategy instruction** - Model thinking, not just assign tasks
4. **Gradual release** - I do → We do → You do together → You do alone
5. **Evidence-based conclusions** - Arguments must cite sources
6. **Metacognitive reflection** - Debrief process, not just content

**All templates require these materials:**

- Compelling or focus question (open-ended)
- Background context (essay, mini-lecture, or timeline)
- Primary sources (minimum 2, maximum 7 for single lesson)
- Graphic organizer (supports thinking skills)
- Formative assessment (embedded or exit ticket)
- Reflection component (metacognitive or content)

**All templates use these pedagogical moves:**

- Think-aloud modeling (make thinking visible)
- Structured collaboration (pairs or small groups before individual work)
- Graphic organizers (externalize cognitive load)
- Scaffolded questions (from simple identification to complex analysis)
- Multiple source perspectives (enable corroboration)

## Implementation Notes for Automated Design

**When generating history lessons, the tool should:**

1. **Detect lesson type from competency:**
   - Keywords trigger templates: "analyze multiple perspectives" → SAC, "understand decisions" → Historical Empathy, "construct argument" → DBQ
   - Default to Template 1 for standard daily lessons

2. **Scale document quantity appropriately:**
   - Check grade level and student proficiency indicators
   - Novice (grade 9-10): 2-4 documents
   - Intermediate (grade 11): 4-6 documents
   - Advanced (grade 12/AP): 6-7 documents

3. **Generate timing estimates based on template:**
   - Use timing tables above
   - Adjust for reading complexity (more time for difficult sources)
   - Flag when lesson exceeds standard period length

4. **Create template-appropriate materials:**
   - Background essay length matches template (300-500 for standard, 500-800 for DBQ)
   - Graphic organizers align to template focus
   - Assessment prompts match template format

5. **Embed scaffolding based on template phase:**
   - Think-aloud script for "I do" phase
   - Guiding questions for "We do" phase
   - Sentence starters for synthesis phase

6. **Quality checks:**
   - Compelling question is genuinely open-ended
   - Documents provide contrasting perspectives
   - Background provides context without answering question
   - Assessment measures historical thinking, not just recall
